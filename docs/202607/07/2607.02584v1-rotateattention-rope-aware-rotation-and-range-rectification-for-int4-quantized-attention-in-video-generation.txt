Abstract. In DiT-based video generation models equipped with 3D Rotary Position Embeddings (3D RoPE) , the attention mech-anism remains a primary computational bottleneck due to its quadratic complexity with respect to sequence length. While quantized FlashAt-tention offers a promising path toward hardware acceleration, exist-ing low-bit quantization methods overlook two critical challenges in this setting: 1) applying online rotation matrices—a widely used technique for mitigating outliers in Queries ( Q) and Keys ( K)—is difficult to reconcile with RoPE ; and 2) the non-negative attention matrix P =exp( QK − max( QK )) makes symmetric quantization waste half of the 4-bit dynamic range. In this work, we observe that the outlier distributions of Q and K are strongly affected by the dimensional partitioning of 3D RoPE . Based on this finding, we propose RotateAttention , an effi-cient mixed-precision INT4 FlashAttention framework tailored for 

DiT-based video generation models with 3D RoPE , using selec-tive FP16 fallback for accuracy-sensitive attention blocks and denoising steps. RotateAttention introduces two core techniques: 1) RoPE-aware Rotation , which employs either mergeable rotation matrices that can be fused into RoPE or negligible-overhead matrices to mitigate RoPE-induced outliers in Q and K; and 2) Range-optimized P Quantiza-tion , which uses fixed scales and zero-points to fully exploit the INT4 numerical range with minimal computational overhead. Experiments show that RotateAttention preserves video generation quality nearly identical to full-precision baselines while achieving up to 1.68 × end-to-end speedup and 2.2 × kernel-level acceleration. 

Keywords: Video generation · Quantized attention · 3D RoPE

Outliers and Rotation Transformation The accuracy of low-bit quantized attention is constrained by the presence of outliers in the tensors—elements whose magnitudes deviate significantly from the mean distribution. These ex-treme values compress the dynamic range available for the majority of the tensor, leading to severe precision loss. Rotation transformations are established tech-niques to mitigate outliers by redistributing tensor values, as discussed in detail in Sec. 2. 

Limitations of Existing Work. Current 4-bit attention methods, when ap-plied to DiT-based video generation models with 3D RoPE, face several limi-tations: 1) The Query ( Q) and Key ( K) matrices are usually not rotated, al-though rotation can effectively equalize outliers. This is because common rota-tion matrices are difficult to fuse with Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE) [31], while online rotation introduces non-negligible overhead. 2) Existing methods employ symmetric quantization for the probability matrix P = exp( S − m).Since P ∈ (0 , 1] , this leaves half of the INT4 representation range unused. Fur-thermore, LLM-oriented quantized attention methods with Q/K rotations do not directly transfer to mixed-precision INT4 attention in 3D-RoPE video DiTs. 

Motivation and Key Findings. In this work, we make the following obser-vations: 1) We investigate the impact of 3D RoPE on the outlier distribution of 

Q and K in 3D-RoPE video DiTs. We identify specific patterns in the outlier distribution that can be exploited to implement a highly efficient and effective rotation matrix. 2) Fully utilizing the 4-bit numerical range for the quantization of P yields a significant improvement in attention accuracy. 

Method. We introduce RotateAttention , which comprises two core innova-tions complemented by a standard offline technique: 1) RoPE-aware Rota-tion: A block-diagonal matrix composed of 2 × 2 orthogonal blocks that effec-tively mitigates outliers in Q and K with minimal overhead, designed to be com-patible with or mergeable into RoPE. 2) Range-Optimized P Quantization:

# 2 Related Work 

Recent advances in quantization [2, 7, 9, 10, 15–20, 22, 30, 32, 34, 35, 53, 55] have significantly improved model efficiency. For matrix multiplication Y = XW ⊤,where X ∈ Rk×n and W ∈ Rm×n, common strategies for maintaining high accuracy include: 

1) Per-channel Scaling. This method [42] mitigates outliers by applying a di-agonal scaling matrix diag (c) to transfer outlier energy from X to W: Y =(Xdiag (c)−1) · (diag (c)W⊤).

2) Rotation Transformation. A more general approach uses a transformation ma-trix H and its inverse transpose H−⊤ such that Y = ( XH )( WH −⊤ )⊤. Orthog-onal rotations (e.g., Hadamard) [1] redistribute outlier energy across channels to reduce maximum values within each matrix ( intra-matrix ). Non-orthogonal invertible transforms act as a composite of rotation and scaling. 

Low-bit Attention in Video Generation. Current low-bit video attention methods focus on quantizing QK ⊤ and PV using alternative techniques: (1) 

QKV Smoothing: The SageAttention series [47, 50] uses INT4/INT8 formats, employing channel smoothing (e.g., Q − E[Q]) to suppress biased outliers. SageAttention3 [49] further extends this line with NVFP4 micro-scaling for-mats. (2) Token Reordering: PAROAttention [54] reorders tokens in Q,

K, and V so that each block of P contains values with similar magnitudes, thereby improving per-block quantization precision. This token-level strategy is 

complementary to our channel-level rotation approach and can potentially be combined for further gains. 

Low-bit Attention in LLMs. LLM quantization typically targets the memory-bound KV cache [21]. To mitigate quantization error, rotation-based methods like FlatQuant and SpinQuant [24, 33] utilize invertible transforms, while QuaRot [1] employs Hadamard matrices. Recent advances, such as FlashAttention-3 [29], further optimize efficiency by reducing rotation overhead within high-performance kernels. 

Key Differences: LLM vs. 3D-RoPE Video DiTs. Fig. 1 illustrates the key differences between LLM decoding and 3D-RoPE video DiT attention, high-lighting why rotation strategies designed for LLM KV-cache quantization do not directly transfer to DiT-based video generation models with 3D RoPE. We identify two fundamental distinctions: (1) Computational Intensity: 

LLM decoding is memory-bound [25,28,57], allowing online rotations to overlap with RoPE or attention kernels. In contrast, 3D-RoPE video DiTs are compute-bound, making online rotation overhead significant and difficult to hide. (2) 

Quantization Symmetry: LLM quantization often keeps Q in FP16 and only quantizes K for the KV-cache. Conversely, 3D-RoPE video DiTs require simulta-neous quantization of Q and K to leverage low-bit matmul hardware for actual speedup. 4 Y. Liu et al.

Furthermore, we extend Hadamard transforms to the Value ( V) matrix. While previously used in LLM KV-caches, we formally prove their compatibil-ity with smoothing for V. By bridging these rotation strategies and enhancing

# 3 RoPE Effect on Outlier Distribution 

Let Q, K, and V denote the query, key, and value matrices, respectively, each belonging to RN ×D , where N represents the sequence length and D denotes the feature dimension. In DiT-based video generation models, 3D RoPE is widely adopted to capture complex spatiotemporal dependencies. Theoretically, the feature dimension D of the Q and K matrices is partitioned into three distinct segments —Df , Dh,and Dw—corresponding to the temporal (frame), height, and width dimensions, respectively, such that D = Df + Dh + Dw.The 3D RoPE rotary matrix M ∈ RD×D is a block-diagonal matrix composed of D/ 2 fundamental rotation blocks Mi ∈ SO(2) . This matrix is partitioned into three sub-matrices— Mf , Mh, and Mw—each responsible for encoding temporal, height, and width information within their respective dimensional segments. 

Implementation. Due to the inherent sparsity of the rotary matrix M, the computation of RoPE substitutes the standard matrix multiplication with a more efficient element-wise operation.

Theoretical Interpretation. Because Q and K share RoPE parameters and are projected from the same hidden states, their channel-wise outlier profiles remain symmetric. 

# 4 Method 

We focus on attention modules in DiT-based video generation models equipped with 3D RoPE. We consider queries ( Q), keys ( K), and values ( V), which are equipped with biases shared across all tokens. To address this, we first perform smoothing on Q, K, and V.Furthermore, we observe that Q and K exhibit specific distributional pat-terns of outliers. Based on these patterns, we apply a specially designed rota-tion for the Query and Key matrices to redistribute and equalize these outliers, thereby improving quantization robustness.

Mathematical Analysis on the Necessity of Orthogonality To maintain computational equivalence, when a transformation matrix R is applied to Q,the matrix K must be transformed by R−⊤ :

QK ⊤ = ( QR )( KR −⊤ )⊤. (5) This equality holds for any invertible R in full precision. However, under low-bit quantization, the transformation not only changes the coordinate system, but also directly affects the numerical ranges and outlier distributions of the transformed Q and K. Since Q and K usually exhibit symmetric and comparable outlier patterns after RoPE, the transformation should preserve their balance rather than suppressing outliers in one matrix while amplifying them in the other. Let R = UΣL ⊤ be the SVD of R, where U, L ∈ O(D) and Σ =diag( σ1, . . . , σ D ). The corresponding transformation on K is 

R−⊤ = (( UΣL ⊤)−1)⊤ = UΣ −1L⊤. (6) Therefore, the singular values applied to K are {1/σ i}Di=1 . If σi < 1, the i-th principal direction of Q is compressed, but the corresponding direction of K is amplified by 1/σ i > 1. Conversely, if σi > 1, the outliers in Q are amplified while those in K are suppressed. In both cases, anisotropic scaling introduces an asymmetric range change between Q and K, which can enlarge the quantization error of either matrix. To avoid this cross-matrix amplification and preserve balanced quantization difficulty, the transformation should impose the same singular scaling on both branches. This requires Σ = Σ−1, i.e., σi = 1 for all i. Consequently, Σ = I and 

R = UL ⊤, which is an orthogonal matrix. This analysis explains why orthogonal rotations are particularly suitable for the symmetric outlier patterns induced by RoPE, as shown in Fig. 2. 

Interleaved Rotation (Merge-able) To integrate with RoPE, we design In-terleaved Rotation . We define a block-diagonal orthogonal matrix R, where each 

2 × 2 block acts on adjacent channel pairs (d2i, d 2i+1 ) for i ∈ { 1, . . . , D/ 2}. This transformation redistributes incoherence, yielding a more uniform outlier profile across the feature dimensions of Q and K.The core advantage of this design is its operator mergeability . Since both the rotary matrix M and our rotation R consist of 2 × 2 orthogonal blocks, their product M′ = RM (or MR ) preserves the block-diagonal structure. Con-sequently, the operations can be fused: 

RoPE (Q)R = Q(MR ), RoPE (K)R = K(MR ). (7) Because MR maintains the same sparsity pattern as M, it remains fully compat-ible with standard element-wise RoPE kernels. By pre-fusing R into the RoPE 8 Y. Liu et al.

# 5 Experiments 

5.1 Experimental Setup 

We evaluate our proposed method on the Wan2.2 [37] T2V and I2V models at 480P resolution with 81 frames (40 sampling steps), and the Hunyuan-Video [13] T2V model at 480P resolution with 129 frames (50 sampling steps). Our evaluation dataset comprises diverse prompts curated from the official Wan2.2, Wan-Lightning [8], and Open-Sora [56] example sets, following the evaluation protocol of SageAttention [50]. For the attention mechanism, we employ per-token quantization for the query ( Q) and key ( K) matrices, and 

per-group quantization for the value ( V) matrix. For Wan2.2-I2V , we preserve FP16 precision for the first 6 and last 4 sam-pling steps, as well as the first and last 2 DiT blocks; all remaining FlashAtten-tion operations are executed with our INT4 attention kernel, resulting in 68% of FlashAttention operations running in INT4. For Wan2.2-T2V , FP16 is main-tained for the first and last 2 sampling steps and DiT blocks, enabling 81% INT4 attention execution. Similarly, for HunyuanVideo-T2V , we keep FP16 for the first 4 and last 2 sampling steps and the first and last 2 DiT blocks, with 76% of attention operations running in INT4. 

Hardware Configuration. We benchmark INT4 attention kernel speed on 

NVIDIA A10 GPUs using custom CUDA kernels, reflecting practical deploy-ment hardware. For accuracy evaluation of the full video generation pipeline, we use NVIDIA H20 GPUs with TileLang [38] operators, which provide precise numerical validation. 

Model Scope. We evaluate on Wan2.2 and HunyuanVideo, two leading open-source video generation models, both employing DiT-based architectures with 3D RoPE. This choice is deliberate: DiT with 3D RoPE has become the de facto standard for state-of-the-art open-source video generation (also adopted by CogVideoX [45], Open-Sora 2.0 [27], and others), making it the most practically relevant setting. 

5.2 Evaluation Metrics 

We primarily employ Relative Difference Metrics to quantify the dis-crepancy between our quantized models and the FP16 baseline. Specifically, 

PSNR [14] and Cosine Similarity measure low-level pixel-space differences, while SSIM [39] evaluates structural similarity. These relative metrics are more RotateAttention 11 

sensitive to the fine-grained artifacts introduced by quantization [54], providing a clearer signal for comparing quantization strategies. We also evaluate absolute video quality using VBench [11], which assesses semantic coherence, temporal consistency, and motion quality. Since VBench scores are less discriminative across quantization variants (methods with visible pixel-level differences often receive similar VBench scores), we use relative met-rics as the primary comparison in the main paper and report VBench results in the supplementary material. 

5.3 Baselines and Ablation Study 

The primary baseline is the standard attention mechanism in FP16 . We also compare against SageAttention [50] with QKV smoothing but without our optimized P and rotation strategies. Furthermore, we evaluate a full rotation baseline using a D × D Hadamard matrix. Extensive ablation studies are performed to analyze the impact of different rotation and optimized P strategies. For the Half Rotation and Interleaved Rotation results in Table 1, the rotation matrix R defaults to the block-diagonal initialization using H2.When applying rotations to Q and K, we include a Hadamard transformation on V by default. Since this transformation is computationally mergeable—and follows standard practice in frameworks like FlatQuant and SpinQuant [24,33]— we adopt it as a default component and omit separate ablation studies for this operation. We note that PAROAttention [54] addresses the complementary problem of token reordering for P quantization, which operates at the token level rather than the channel level. Our rotation strategy and PAROAttention’s reorder-ing are orthogonal techniques that can be combined. We provide a software-level comparison and combination experiment in the supplementary material, demon-strating additive accuracy gains when both approaches are applied. 

Learned Rotation Setup. As a preliminary investigation into learned rotations (see Sec. 4.1), we train under two conditions: (1) Unconstrained and (2) Orthogonal . We use Half Rotation with H2 initialization on Wan2.2-I2V and 1–2 calibration samples.

Range-Optimized P Quantization yields clear improvements on Wan2.2-T2V and HunyuanVideo across all metrics, yet provides marginal gains on Wan2.2-I2V. We attribute this to the I2V model’s image conditioning, which constrains the attention distribution P to be sharply peaked (low entropy) with values concentrated near 0 and 1. In this regime, standard symmetric quan-tization effectively preserves the dominant structure, while range-optimized quantization can introduce subtle precision degradation. In contrast, T2V mod-els produce more diffuse P distributions that benefit substantially from the full INT4 dynamic range. These findings suggest that range-optimized quantization is most beneficial for moderate-to-high entropy attention, and may be disabled for strongly conditioned, low-entropy patterns. 

Analysis of Rotation Strategy Differences. Performance differences match RoPE-induced outlier structure: Half Rotation is strongest when outliers con-centrate in one half-segment (I2V and HunyuanVideo), while Interleaved Ro-tation is better when local adjacent-channel correlation dominates (T2V). Full Rotation can over-smooth useful local structure despite stronger global redis-tribution. 

Practical recommendation: For deployment on new models, we recom-mend Half Rotation as the default strategy due to its consistent performance across all tested configurations. Interleaved Rotation offers a compelling zero-cost alternative when it can be pre-fused into RoPE weights, particularly for T2V applications. Visual inspection in Fig. 4 reveals that for T2V tasks, Interleaved and Half Rotation occasionally outperform Full Rotation in specific cases. Moreover, as shown in Figs. 4 and 5, both strategies exhibit clear visual superiority over 14 Y. Liu et al. 

the SageAttention baseline, particularly in maintaining structural integrity and temporal consistency. 

Learnable Rotation Result. Learned rotation is effective only under strict orthogonality constraints, as shown in Fig. 6; unconstrained training degrades quality (Sec. 4.1), confirming that LLM-style unconstrained rotations [24, 33] do not transfer directly to 3D-RoPE video DiTs. With 1–2 calibration samples, orthogonal learned rotation does not outperform fixed Half Rotation. 

Speed and Cost Analysis. Our INT4 kernel yields 2.2 × speedup versus cus-tom FP16 attention, and end-to-end speedups of 1.51 × (Wan2.2-I2V), 1.68 ×

(Wan2.2-T2V), and 1.61 × (HunyuanVideo). Full rotation scales linearly with sequence length, whereas attention scales quadratically. Consequently, for se-quences exceeding 30k tokens, full rotation overhead remains under 1% of at-tention computation. However, when paired with sparse attention on shorter sequences ( <10k tokens), its relative overhead increases to over 10% of the at-tention computation. Notably, Half Rotation reduces this overhead to approxi-mately 3% of the full rotation cost. 

# 6 Conclusion 

In this work, we propose RotateAttention , an efficient mixed-precision INT4 attention framework designed for DiT-based video generation models with 3D RoPE. Our approach introduces two core innovations: (1) RoPE-aware Rota-tion , which utilizes mergeable or negligible-cost rotation matrices to effectively mitigate Q and K outliers while maintaining seamless integration with RoPE; (2) Range-optimized P Quantization , which leverages fixed scales and zero-points to fully exploit the INT4 numerical range with minimal computational overhead. Additionally, we apply a standard Hadamard transform to V, fused offline into the linear projections at zero inference cost. Experimental results demonstrate that RotateAttention achieves video generation quality nearly identical to full-precision baselines and significantly outperforms SageAttention in visual fidelity. Our INT4 attention kernel achieves a 2.2× speedup at the kernel level, translating to end-to-end speedups of 1.51 ×–

1.68 × across the evaluated 3D-RoPE video DiTs. RotateAttention establishes a practical paradigm for high-performance, mixed-precision INT4 attention in 3D-RoPE video DiTs. 

Limitations. Our rotation strategies are specifically designed for DiT-based video generation models employing 3D RoPE with segmented dimensional par-titioning. Applying them to other positional encoding schemes requires adapting the rotation structure to the corresponding outlier patterns. Our evaluation cov-ers two model families, Wan2.2 and HunyuanVideo; evaluating a broader range of architectures would further strengthen generalizability. RotateAttention 15
